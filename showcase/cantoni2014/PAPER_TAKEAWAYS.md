main_argument: China's staggered high-school politics curriculum reform causally shifted elite students' political and economic attitudes toward several government-desired positions.

core_mechanism: A sharp province-by-cohort curriculum switch changed textbook and gaokao-tested content, exposing adjacent student cohorts to different official messages about government legitimacy, socialist democracy, and the state role in markets.

key_numbers:
- Survey sample: nearly 2,000 Peking University students; Table 2 reports 1,954 observations, with 619 old-curriculum and 1,335 new-curriculum students.
- Trust in government index: +0.253 standard deviations, p = 0.001.
- Democracy / political participation index: +0.220 standard deviations, p = 0.003.
- Skepticism of market economy: +8.7 percentage points, p = 0.034.
- Minorities, Han-student index: +0.089 standard deviations, p = 0.263.
- Minorities, non-Han-student index: +0.493 standard deviations, p = 0.118.
- Environment index: -0.162 standard deviations, p = 0.104.
- Median persuasion rates reported by the paper: 23% unconditional and 20% conditional.

candidate_highlights:
- Staggered province-by-cohort rollout between 2004 and 2010 provides the identification structure.
- Effects are strongest and statistically sharp for trust in government, democracy / participation, and market skepticism.
- Minority identity outcomes generally move in the intended direction but are less statistically compelling.
- Environmental attitudes do not move in the desired direction.
- Teaching style did not change materially, supporting the interpretation that content rather than pedagogy drove the results.

selected_highlights_for_figure:
- Main structure: a curriculum switch produces a three-part ideological shift.
- Keep three primary effects: trust, democracy / participation, and market skepticism.
- Keep one boundary result: environment fails to move in the desired direction.
- Use the median persuasion rate as a compact magnitude anchor.

deferred_highlights:
