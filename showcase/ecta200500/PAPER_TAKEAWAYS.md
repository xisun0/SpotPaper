## Main Argument

A large-scale randomized cash transfer program (GiveDirectly, USD 1,000 PPP per household, ≈16% of local GDP) in rural Kenya generates aggregate economic effects that substantially exceed the direct transfer amount. The key contribution is the first experimental estimate of a local fiscal multiplier, documented via unusually comprehensive data on recipients, non-recipients, enterprises, and prices.

## Core Mechanism

External cash → recipient spending → local enterprise revenue (demand-led, not investment-led) → wage/profit gains for non-recipient households → further spending → local GDP amplification. The chain is demand-driven; enterprise investment does not increase meaningfully, but enterprise revenue and wage bills rise substantially.

## Candidate Highlights

1. **Symmetric spillover**: Non-recipients gain almost as much in consumption as recipients ($335 vs $339) — the spillover "aha" moment
2. **Multiplier = 2.5**: Each dollar transferred generates $2.50 in local economic output
3. **Demand-led, not inflation**: Enterprise revenue rises but profit margins fall; output price inflation ≈ 0.1%
4. **Wage channel**: Non-recipient gains come primarily from wages (+$183/yr) not asset drawdown
5. **82% from non-recipients**: Most of the household contribution to the multiplier comes from spillover households
